# Demo: Fallacy Identification Challenge

Practice spotting logical fallacies in real-world arguments.

## Exercise 1: Social Media Arguments

Identify the fallacies in these posts:

**Post A**: "You can't trust Professor Johnson's research on climate change because he drives an SUV."

**Fallacy**: Ad Hominem - attacking the person rather than addressing the research.

**Post B**: "If we allow students to use calculators in math class, pretty soon they won't be able to do any math at all in their heads!"

**Fallacy**: Slippery Slope - assuming one change will inevitably lead to extreme consequences.

**Post C**: "Either you support this new tax policy, or you don't care about education."

**Fallacy**: False Dilemma - presenting only two options when more exist.

## Exercise 2: Advertisement Analysis

Find the fallacies in these marketing claims:

1. "9 out of 10 dentists recommend our toothpaste!" (What information is missing?)
2. "This diet worked for me, so it will work for you too!"
3. "Our product is the best because it's the most popular!"

## Exercise 3: Construct Sound Arguments

Take these fallacious arguments and rebuild them properly:

**Original**: "We shouldn't listen to Maria's proposal because she's only been here for six months."

**Improved**: "Let's evaluate Maria's proposal on its merits. While she's new, we should also consider how her fresh perspective might offer valuable insights we haven't considered."

## Discussion

- Why are fallacies so common in everyday discourse?
- How can we avoid fallacies in our own reasoning?
- What's the difference between a fallacy and a persuasive technique?
